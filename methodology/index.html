Scoring methodology

Everything about how NORM measures the gap between what brands claim and what they visibly support — published in full. Any brand, researcher, or journalist can audit this. Nothing is hidden.

What this methodology measures: the alignment between public-facing marketing claims and visibly available supporting information at the time of scan. It does not assess product efficacy, safety, or regulatory status. It does not assume intent.

Overview

NORM measures the gap between what wellness brands publicly claim, what evidence they visibly provide, and how clearly those claims are connected to that evidence. The NORM Rating (0–100) reflects that alignment — AI-proposed, gate-enforced, recalculated on every scan.

Green Rating
68 – 100
Claims appear proportionate to available evidence. Visible support is present and clearly connected.
Yellow Rating
40 – 67
Evidence exists, but gaps are present between what is claimed and what is visibly supported.
Red Rating
0 – 39
Claims extend materially beyond visible support. The gap between what is claimed and what is shown is significant.

How scoring works

The scoring engine uses a hybrid architecture: an AI analysis layer proposes a score across six dimensions, and a deterministic gate layer can only lower that score — never raise it. No gate can be overridden by AI output.

// Step 1: AI analysis layer
ai_proposed_score = analyze(claim_packet, evidence_signals, category)
// Haiku first pass (~$0.001/brand). Sonnet escalation for uncertain / high-risk.

// Step 2: Deterministic gate layer
risk_floor = evaluate_gates(tier1_claims, evidence_signals, regulatory_language, confidence)
max_allowed = 100 − risk_floor

final_score = min(ai_proposed_score, max_allowed)
// Gates are decisions. AI output is a proposal. Higher score = better alignment.

Deterministic gates

Gates fire based on observable signals in the claim data — not AI judgment. When a gate fires, the score cannot exceed the gate's ceiling regardless of what AI analysis returns.

// G1: Outcome claims present, zero evidence signals → max score: 28
if has_outcome_claims AND no_evidence: risk_floor = 72

// G2: Outcome claims + some evidence, but no studies or certifications → max score: 42
if has_outcome_claims AND has_evidence AND no_studies AND no_certs: risk_floor = 58

// G4: Regulatory language (treats / prevents / cures / FDA-approved) → max score: 35
if has_regulatory_language: risk_floor = 65

// G6: Low extraction confidence (fewer than 6 claims captured) → max score: 60
if confidence == "low": risk_floor = 40

// Multiple gates: take the highest floor
final_floor = max(all triggered floors)

Worked example

A supplement brand with ingredient-level studies on a /science page, a "clinically proven" hero claim, but no product-level trial and no third-party certification.

Mechanism             8 / 15  // delivery rationale mentioned

Six dimensions

Each dimension contributes a specific point value to the total score (0–100). AI analysis proposes each dimension score; the deterministic gate layer may cap the total but does not modify individual dimension scores.

DimensionMax ptsWhat it measures
Specificity15Are ingredients named with specific doses, forms, and sources? Exact mg/mcg/IU doses, named bioavailable forms (methylfolate vs folic acid, D3 vs D2, MK-7 vs K2), and disclosed ingredient sources score highest. Vague "proprietary blend" with no disclosed amounts scores near zero.
Mechanism15Does the brand explain how claimed effects occur biologically? Credit for delivery system rationale (liposomal, enteric coating, chelation), form-selection reasoning (why D3 over D2), and device mechanism description. Bioavailability claims establish how a nutrient is absorbed — mechanism credit, not evidence of clinical outcomes.
Evidence Presence10Are there any visible evidence signals at all? Linked PubMed studies, DOI journal links, dedicated science pages with named studies, third-party certifications (NSF, USP, Informed Sport, USDA Organic, GMP), and brand-run clinical trials with disclosed methodology each contribute. Subscription-gated content does not count — only what a consumer can verify publicly.
Evidence Strength25The quality gate. Product-level RCTs on the actual marketed product score highest. Ingredient-level peer-reviewed studies at relevant doses earn moderate credit. Internal brand studies, small samples (<50), bioavailability data, and testimonials earn near-zero. Critical check: does the cited evidence directly support the specific marketed claim? Evidence that doesn't match the claim is scored accordingly.
Effect Reality15Do claimed effects match what published science generally supports for this product type, category, and ingredient class? Claims that exceed what the literature shows for the category score lower. Plausible claims consistent with established science score higher.
Claim Proportionality20How proportionate is the marketing language to the visible evidence base? Measured, qualified language that accurately represents what evidence shows scores highest. Claims that extend materially beyond cited evidence score lowest — including clinical language for weak studies, treatment-adjacent claims, or narrow findings extrapolated to broad consumer promises.

Claim placement context

Every extracted claim is tagged with where it appeared on the page. Placement context is passed to the analysis layer because a "clinically proven" claim in the hero headline carries different weight than the same phrase in fine-print footer copy — that's where brands put the claims they most want you to believe.

Category-specific rules

Each product category has additional analysis rules applied during scoring. These rules flag patterns specific to the category — they are qualitative checks baked into the AI analysis layer, not separate mathematical additions to the score.

Cross-category rules applied to every scan:
  • Dose non-disclosure — if a product makes specific outcome claims (skin glow, mood boost, energy, craving control, recovery) but does not disclose the amount of each active functional ingredient per serving, this is flagged. "Contains collagen peptides" with no mg amount + a skin claim = an unverifiable claim by design.
  • Ingredient presence ≠ ingredient effect — listing an ingredient does not substantiate its claimed effect. The scoring system requires that the ingredient, form, and dose all be visible and connected to the specific claim.
  • Evidence-claim distance — evidence that doesn't directly address the marketed claim is scored for what it is. Ingredient-level studies used to back finished-product claims receive lower credit than product-level evidence.
  • Regulatory language — language that implies disease treatment, prevention, or cure ("treats", "prevents", "reverses", FDA-adjacent framing) triggers an elevated risk floor regardless of evidence present.

Supplements

  • No Supplement Facts panel visible, or individual active amounts hidden in proprietary blends
  • No third-party testing certificate visible (NSF, Informed Sport, USP, COA)
  • Ingredient-level evidence used in place of finished-product evidence
  • No author names or citation links visible alongside study claims

Functional food & beverages

  • Energy, cognitive, or mood claims require visible studies at the delivered dose — ingredient-level evidence alone is not sufficient
  • Mood booster or craving control as a product name or hero claim is treated as an implicit health claim and requires ingredient-level evidence at disclosed doses

Mental wellness

  • Anxiety, stress, or mood claims carry elevated regulatory risk sensitivity
  • Adaptogen efficacy claims require visible human studies at relevant doses

Skincare

  • Dermatologist-tested language without citations
  • Clinical claims without visible study references
  • Before/after photo use as clinical proof without referenced methodology
  • SPF claims without visible testing references

Fitness & performance

  • Strength or endurance claims without product-specific human trials
  • Proprietary blends claiming specific performance outcomes without individual ingredient disclosure
  • Device accuracy claims (calorie burn, muscle activation) without third-party validation against clinical reference standards

Sleep aids

  • Melatonin dosage claims should align with visible Supplement Facts
  • Sleep stage improvement claims (REM, deep sleep) require polysomnography or validated tracking data
  • Device-based sleep claims without product-specific validation

Neurotech & brain devices

  • Stimulation parameters not disclosed (Hz, mA, duration, waveform)
  • EEG or neurofeedback accuracy claims without visible validation
  • Medical vs. wellness boundary language without appropriate disclaimer

Recovery devices (red light, PEMF, cold therapy, massage)

  • Wavelength, irradiance, or protocol not specified on page
  • Product-specific evidence missing — ingredient evidence ≠ device evidence
  • Comparison to medical devices without substantiation

Wearables & trackers

  • Sensor accuracy % claims without visible validation studies
  • Algorithm or AI-based insight claims without transparency

Confidence score

Separate from the NORM Rating, confidence reflects how reliably the scan captured the page's content. Low confidence triggers a "Limited Data" state and activates the G6 gate, capping the score at 60 regardless of AI analysis.

  • High — page fully extracted, claims clearly identifiable, content complete
  • Medium — partial extraction or some ambiguity in claim language
  • Low — thin content, JavaScript-heavy page, or insufficient signal to score reliably

Ranking

Within each category, products are sorted by NORM Rating descending — highest score = Rank #1. Tie-breakers in order:

  • Higher confidence score
  • More product-specific evidence visible
  • Better source transparency
  • More recent scan

Low-confidence scans are placed at the bottom with "Rank Pending" until confidence improves.

Score thresholds & verdict labels

The NORM Rating maps to three colors. The rating does not tell consumers what to buy — it shows what holds.

  • Green (68–100) — claims appear proportionate to available evidence. Visible support is present and clearly connected.
  • Yellow (40–67) — evidence exists, but gaps are present between what is claimed and what is visibly supported.
  • Red (0–39) — claims extend materially beyond visible support. The gap between what is claimed and what is shown is significant.

Within each color band, a plain-language verdict label describes the specific alignment pattern found:

The role of AI

NORM uses a hybrid architecture: AI proposes scores, deterministic gates enforce ceilings. They are sequenced, not blended — a triggered gate cannot be overridden by AI output.

AI layer (Claude): analyzes the full claim packet and proposes a score across six dimensions. Haiku handles the first pass (~$0.001/brand); Sonnet escalates automatically for uncertain, high-risk, or low-confidence scores.

Gate layer: fires on observable signals — outcome claim presence, evidence types, regulatory language, extraction confidence — setting a maximum the AI score cannot exceed.

All AI output is validated against NORM's language requirements. The AI cannot speculate about intent or use verdict language like: fraud, scam, fake, false, illegal, deceptive, noncompliant, or "doesn't work."

V1 scope and maturity

NORM v1 establishes the foundational framework: a consistent, reproducible way to measure the distance between what a brand claims and what it visibly shows. The scoring dimensions, evidence hierarchy, and claim tier system are designed to be stable — but the specific thresholds, gate values, and verdict bands are calibrated starting points, not final authority.

What v1 is built to do well

  • Detect clear evidence gaps: brands making clinical-strength claims with no linked evidence
  • Reward transparency: brands that cite studies, disclose ingredient forms, and show third-party testing
  • Create a consistent baseline across categories that can be tracked over time

What v1 does not yet claim

  • Optimal threshold calibration across every product category — gate floors and verdict bands will be refined as scored brands and appeals data accumulate
  • Category-specific depth — a recovery device and a cognitive supplement are measured against the same framework today; category-specific weighting is in the roadmap
  • Longitudinal authority — one scoring run is a snapshot; confidence in verdicts increases as brands are rescored across multiple cycles

How it improves

Brand appeals that surface missed evidence, editorial review of outlier scores, and category expansion each trigger versioned updates. Gate structure is stable — only floor values and weight distributions are refined.

What NORM does not measure

NORM is not a review platform. NORM is not a medical authority. NORM is not a product efficacy judge.

NORM measures the gap between what brands claim, what evidence they provide, and how clearly those claims are connected to that evidence. Nothing more.

A red rating means claims extend beyond visible support on the page at the time of scan — not that a product is unsafe, ineffective, or that any intent to mislead exists.
  • Product efficacy — whether the product delivers its promised outcome
  • Clinical safety — whether ingredients or devices are safe to use
  • Ingredient quality — whether underlying ingredients are effective at any dose
  • Manufacturing standards — GMP compliance, facility audits, contamination risk
  • Regulatory compliance — whether claims violate FTC, FDA, or other regulations
  • Company intent — NORM does not assume or assess intent

Disputes & corrections

If a score is wrong because a /science page was missed, a claim was misread, or the page has changed, brands and third parties can request a correction.

How to dispute a score

  • Email support@trynorm.co with the subject line Score Dispute — [domain]
  • Describe what was missed or misrepresented and where the correct evidence is publicly accessible
  • Scores are updated only when the supporting evidence is verifiably visible on the live consumer-facing page — not when evidence is emailed to us
  • We aim to acknowledge dispute emails within 5 business days
What we correct: crawling errors, missed public evidence pages, outdated scans. What we do not change based on disputes: methodology weights, scoring thresholds, or how the system categorizes claim intensity. Those are fixed rules, published above.

Update cadence

  • New scans — immediate provisional rank on first scan
  • High-traffic categories (supplements, skincare, recovery) — ranks recalculated hourly
  • All other categories — ranks recalculated daily
  • Brand evidence submissions — reviewed by humans; scores update only when evidence becomes visible on the live consumer-facing page
  • Cache TTL — 6 hours for known pages, 1 hour for new entries

Questions about the methodology? Email support@trynorm.co.
